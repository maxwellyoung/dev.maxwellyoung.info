I build things that don't make people feel bad.

That's a weird mission statement but it's the one I keep coming back to. Most apps — especially health and productivity apps — are built on anxiety. Streaks that punish you for missing. Progress bars that make failure visible. Notifications engineered to create compulsion.

I'm more interested in the other direction: interfaces that make the right choice feel obvious. Environments that reduce friction rather than weaponize it.

## What I do

Design engineering. That means I'm equally comfortable in Figma and in a TypeScript file. I care about the seam between design and code — the place where motion, interaction, and system design meet.

Right now I'm:
- Building the React Native mobile client for [Silk](https://silk.cx) — a social platform that rejects engagement metrics
- Shipping iOS apps under my own name: Vape Quit Coach (4.8★), Doomscroll, Good News Bad News, Dry Club, Holdspace
- Finishing a CS masters at AUT, building [Liner](https://liner.ninetynine.digital) as the R&D project — a spatial canvas for organizing music
- Doing client work through [ninetynine.digital](https://ninetynine.digital)

## Why iOS

There's a specific kind of care that goes into a good native app. The physics of a swipe gesture. The weight of a haptic. The way an animation communicates "this action happened." You can't fake it with a web wrapper and you can't automate it with a framework. It requires taste.

I also like the constraint. A phone screen is small and your user's attention is limited. Every element has to earn its place.
